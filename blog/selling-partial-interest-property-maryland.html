What Is a Partial Interest in Property?

A partial interest means you own a percentage of a property rather than the whole thing. Instead of one person holding full title, ownership is split among two or more parties. These fractional shares are expressed as percentages â for example, two siblings might each own 50%, or four heirs might each hold 25%.

Partial interests are created in several common ways:

  • Inheritance â A parent or grandparent passes away and leaves the property to multiple children or family members, often without a will.
  • Tenants in common (TIC) â Two or more people own a property together, each holding a defined percentage. Unlike joint tenancy, these shares do not have to be equal and do not automatically transfer when one owner dies.
  • Joint tenancy â Co-owners hold equal shares with a right of survivorship, meaning when one owner passes, their share automatically goes to the surviving owners.
  • Divorce or separation â When a couple splits, one party may retain a partial interest in the marital home.
  • Business partnerships â Investment properties held by multiple partners where one wants to exit.

Why Partial Interests Are So Hard to Sell

If you have tried to sell a partial interest through a traditional real estate agent, you have probably discovered how difficult it is. Partial interest sales are exceedingly rare on the open market. There are several reasons why:

  • Limited control â A buyer purchasing a 50% interest in a property cannot make decisions alone. Renovations, repairs, renting, or selling the whole property all require agreement from the other owner or owners. Most buyers are not willing to take on that complication.
  • Financing is nearly impossible â Banks typically refuse to finance partial interest purchases. A lender cannot foreclose on half a house if the borrower defaults, so most banks simply will not touch it. This means the buyer pool is limited to cash-only purchasers.
  • Discount is required â Because of the limited control and financing challenges, partial interests sell at a significant discount compared to their proportional value. If a property is worth $300,000 and you own 50%, your share is not worth $150,000 on the open market â it could be worth considerably less.
  • Extended time on market â These properties can sit unsold for a year or more, further driving down the price.

You Have More Options Than You Think

Many people who own a partial interest in a Maryland property feel stuck. They assume that because they do not own the whole property, they cannot do anything. That is not true. Under Maryland law, you have the legal right to sell your ownership share without the permission of other co-owners.

Here are your options:

Option 1: Sell to the Other Owners

The most straightforward path is selling your share to the other co-owners. This is how many partial interest situations resolve â the person who lives in the property or who wants to keep it buys out the others. However, this requires the other owners to have the cash or financing to make it happen, and you still need to agree on a price.

Option 2: Partition Action

If co-owners cannot agree, Maryland law allows you to file a partition action in court. The court can order the property sold and the proceeds divided. However, partition actions are expensive, time-consuming, and adversarial â they can damage family relationships and drag on for months.
