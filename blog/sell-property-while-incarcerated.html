You Can Sell Your House or Property Interest From Jail or Prison

Own a house, land, or a share of a property? You don’t have to wait until you get out. Here’s how to turn your property into cash.

Home › Blog › Sell Property While Incarcerated

Published April 18, 2026 • Pages of Purpose LLC

If you’re reading this from inside a correctional facility, I’m going to get straight to the point. You may own a house, land, or a share of a property that you can sell for real money — even while you’re locked up. A lot of people don’t even realize they have this option. Some don’t know they’re still on a deed. Some inherited a piece of a property years ago and forgot about it. Some just assumed they couldn’t do anything about it until they got out.

That’s not true. You have legal rights to your property whether you’re incarcerated or not. Being locked up doesn’t take away your ownership. And if you need money — for commissary, legal fees, family support, or just to have something saved up for when you come home — selling your property or your share of a property is a real option that people exercise more often than you’d think.

This guide is written for you. Not your family member, not your attorney — you. Here’s what you need to know.

You Still Own Your Property

Let’s start with the most important thing. Going to jail or prison does not take away your property rights. If your name is on a deed — whether it’s a house, a mobile home on land, a vacant lot, or a piece of family property — you still own it. The state can’t just take it because you’re incarcerated.

There are only a few situations where you could lose property: if there’s a court-ordered forfeiture tied to your case (this mostly applies to properties involved in certain federal drug cases), if the property is sold at a tax sale because nobody’s been paying the taxes, or if the mortgage goes into foreclosure because the payments stopped.

But in most cases, your name is still on that deed and you have every right to sell.

Do You Even Know What You Own?

This is a bigger issue than most people realize. A lot of people who are incarcerated own property and don’t even know it. Here are common situations I see:

You inherited a share of a family property. When a parent or grandparent dies without a will, their property passes to their children automatically under state law. That means you might own a third, a quarter, or some other fraction of a family home or piece of land — even if you never signed anything, never lived there, and never thought about it.

You’re still on a deed from a previous relationship. Maybe you bought a house with an ex before you got locked up. Even if they’ve been living there and paying the mortgage for years, if your name is still on the deed, you still have an ownership interest.

You own land you forgot about. Maybe a family member put a lot or a piece of rural property in your name years ago. Maybe you bought a vacant lot before your situation changed. If it’s still in your name, it’s still yours.

You own a mobile home. In many states, mobile homes are treated as personal property, not real estate. But if you own the mobile home and the land it sits on, that’s real property you can sell.

How to find out what you own: Have someone on the outside check the county land records where you lived or where your family has property. Most counties have free online property searches. You can also write to the county recorder’s office and ask them to search your name. If you know the address, that makes it even easier.

Selling Your Whole Property

If you’re the sole owner — meaning only your name is on the deed — you can sell the entire property. You don’t need anyone else’s permission. Here’s how it works from inside:

You sign a Power of Attorney. This is a legal document that gives someone you trust — a family member, a friend, or an attorney — the legal authority to handle the sale on your behalf. They can sign the paperwork, negotiate the price, and close the deal for you. Most correctional facilities have a notary on staff who can notarize the POA. You’ll need to contact your facility’s legal services or administration office to arrange it.

You work with a buyer who understands your situation. This is important. Not every buyer or real estate agent knows how to handle a sale when the owner is incarcerated. Some title companies won’t even touch it. You want to work with someone who’s done this before — typically a cash home buyer who’s experienced with complicated title situations. They know which title companies will close these deals and how to keep the process moving.

The sale closes, and you get paid. The proceeds from the sale go wherever you direct them. That could be your commissary account, a bank account in your name, a family member, or a trust. The title company handles the distribution at closing based on your instructions (which you set up through your POA or directly).

Selling Your Share of a Property

This is the part most people don’t realize is possible. If you own a partial interest in a property — even a small one — you can sell just your share. You don’t need the other owners to agree. You don’t need to sell the whole property. You can sell your piece and walk away with your money.

Here’s a common example. Say your mother passed away and left a house to you and your two siblings. You each own a one-third share. Your siblings are living in the house or managing it. You’re incarcerated and getting nothing from the property. You can sell your one-third interest to a buyer — typically an investor or a cash buyer who specializes in partial interests — and get cash for your share.

Another example: you and your ex bought a house together, 50/50. You’re now locked up and they’re living in the house. You can sell your 50% interest. The buyer steps into your position as co-owner alongside your ex.

What’s Your Share Actually Worth?

Your share is based on the fair market value of the whole property, divided by your ownership percentage. But there’s usually a discount when you’re selling a partial interest rather than the whole property, because the buyer is taking on the complication of being a co-owner with other people.

For example, if the property is worth $150,000 and you own one-third, your share is technically worth $50,000. In practice, a buyer might offer you $25,000 to $35,000 for that interest, depending on the situation — who else is on the deed, whether the property generates rent, what condition it’s in, and how cooperative the other owners are.

That might sound like a steep discount, but think about it this way: that’s $25,000 to $35,000 you didn’t have before, from a property that was doing nothing for you while you’re inside. And you get it relatively fast — typically within a few weeks of starting the process.

What If the Other Owners Don’t Want You to Sell?

Here’s the straight answer: they don’t get to stop you. You own your share. It’s your legal right to sell it. They might not like it, and it might cause family tension, but legally you have every right to sell your interest in the property.

That said, the smart move is usually to give the other owners a chance to buy you out first. If your sibling wants to keep the family home, they might prefer to pay you for your share rather than end up co-owning the property with an outside investor. This can be a cleaner deal for everyone.

If they won’t buy you out and you sell to an outside buyer, the new co-owner can eventually force a sale of the whole property through something called a partition action. This is a court process where any co-owner can petition to have the property sold and the proceeds split. The other owners would get their share too — but the property would be sold.

Bottom line: Your share is yours. Whether you sell it to a family member, an outside investor, or force a sale through partition — you have options, and none of them require you to wait until you’re released.

How the Paperwork Actually Works From Inside

I’m not going to pretend the logistics are simple. Getting legal documents signed in a correctional facility takes some coordination. But it’s done all the time. Here’s the realistic process:

Step 1: Contact your facility’s legal services. Ask about their process for notarizing legal documents. Find out if they have a notary on staff and what the scheduling looks like. Some facilities handle this quickly; others have a waiting list.

Step 2: Get a Power of Attorney drafted. You want a limited or specific POA that names someone you trust and specifically authorizes the sale of your property at a specific address. A general POA works too, but title companies prefer the specific version. An attorney can draft this for you, or the buyer you’re working with can often help coordinate it.

Step 3: Sign and notarize the POA. The notary at your facility witnesses your signature. This makes the document legally valid. Make sure your person on the outside gets the original notarized copy — most transactions require the original, not a photocopy.

Step 4: Your POA holder handles the sale. Once the POA is in place, your designated person can accept an offer, sign the contract, and close the deal. You don’t need to be present for any of it. The title company processes the sale, pays off any liens or mortgage, and distributes the remaining proceeds according to your instructions.

What if you can’t get a POA signed? In some facilities or situations, it’s difficult to get legal documents processed. If that happens, some buyers will actually come to the facility to meet with you directly. I’ve personally worked with sellers who were incarcerated — arranging visits, coordinating with facility staff, and making sure everything gets signed properly. It takes more effort, but it gets the job done.

Where Does the Money Go?

This is one of the first questions people ask, and it’s a fair one. When you sell a property from inside, you control where the proceeds go. Common options:

Direct deposit to your commissary account. Some buyers and title companies can arrange a direct deposit to your facility account. This depends on the facility’s policies — not all of them accept outside deposits the same way.

Into a bank account. If you still have a bank account open, the title company can wire or mail a check to that account. If you don’t have one, your POA holder can open one on your behalf (depending on the bank’s policies) or hold the funds in a trust.

To a family member. You can direct the proceeds to be paid to a family member. Just make sure this is clearly documented — you don’t want any confusion about who was supposed to get what.

Held for your release. Some people choose to have the money held in a trust or savings account so it’s waiting for them when they get out. This is smart if you’re getting out in the near future and want a financial cushion for your transition home.

Important: If you have any court-ordered restitution, fines, or child support obligations, some or all of the sale proceeds could be subject to liens or garnishment. You should talk to an attorney about your specific situation before selling so you know exactly what you’ll walk away with. It’s still usually better to sell and deal with the obligations than to let the property sit there losing value or getting taken at a tax sale.

Properties People Don’t Realize They Can Sell

When I say “property,” I don’t just mean a nice house in the suburbs. People sell all kinds of property from inside. Here’s what’s fair game:

Houses — in any condition. It doesn’t matter if the house needs work, is vacant, has code violations, or hasn’t been maintained. Cash buyers purchase properties as-is. You don’t need to fix anything.

Vacant land. Lots, acreage, rural land — if your name is on the deed, you can sell it. Land is often easier to sell than houses because there are fewer complications with condition or maintenance.

Mobile homes with land. If you own both the mobile home and the land underneath it, that’s a sellable asset. Even older mobile homes on owned land have value.

Inherited property shares. Like I mentioned earlier, if someone in your family died and left property, you may own a share without even knowing it. This is especially common in families where property passed down without wills.

Property in other states. You don’t have to be incarcerated in the same state where the property is located. A POA works across state lines. If you own property in Maryland but you’re locked up in Virginia, you can still sell.

What Happens If You Do Nothing?

This is worth talking about because a lot of people choose to just wait until they get out. Sometimes that makes sense — if you’re getting released in a few months and you have someone trustworthy maintaining the property, waiting might be fine.

But if you’re in for a longer stretch, doing nothing can cost you everything:

Property taxes pile up. Every year you’re inside, the county is adding tax bills. After a few years of unpaid taxes, the county can sell your property at a tax sale for pennies on the dollar. You lose the property entirely and get nothing.

The house deteriorates. Vacant homes fall apart fast. Pipes burst, roofs leak, squatters move in, the city sends code violations. After a few years of neglect, a property that was worth $150,000 might only be worth $50,000 — or less.

The mortgage goes to foreclosure. If nobody’s making mortgage payments, the bank will eventually foreclose. You lose the property, your credit gets destroyed, and depending on your state, you might still owe the deficiency.

Family members make decisions without you. If you’re not actively involved, someone else might sell the property, rent it out, or make decisions about it without your consent. Getting ahead of this by making your own decision protects your interests.

The point is: your property is either an asset or a liability. If it’s sitting there costing money in taxes and maintenance with nobody using it, it’s a liability. Turning it into cash makes it an asset again.
