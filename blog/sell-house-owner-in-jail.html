How to Sell a House When the Owner Is in Jail or Prison

A step-by-step guide covering Power of Attorney, court orders, and your fastest options for getting the property sold.

Home › Blog › Sell House — Owner Incarcerated

Published April 18, 2026 • Pages of Purpose LLC

Nobody plans for this. One day your husband, your mother, your brother, or your co-owner is arrested — and suddenly you’re stuck with a house you can’t afford, can’t maintain, and can’t figure out how to sell. The mortgage is still due. The taxes are still accruing. And the person whose name is on the deed is behind bars.

If that’s where you are right now, take a breath. You can sell a house when the owner is incarcerated. It takes some extra legal steps, but it’s absolutely doable — and people do it more often than you’d think. This guide walks you through exactly how, in plain English.

Why This Situation Is So Stressful

Most homeowners dealing with an incarcerated property owner are also dealing with about five other crises at the same time. Maybe you’re trying to cover the mortgage on a single income. Maybe the house is sitting vacant and the city is sending code violation notices. Maybe there are back taxes piling up. And on top of all that, you’re navigating the emotional weight of having someone you care about locked up.

The house becomes one more problem on the pile. And the worst part is that most real estate agents, title companies, and even some attorneys aren’t familiar with how to handle it. So you get passed around, told “I’m not sure,” or given advice that doesn’t actually apply to your situation.

Here’s what actually works.

Step 1: Figure Out Who’s on the Deed

Before anything else, you need to know whose name is on the property deed — not the mortgage, the deed. These are two different things. The deed determines who legally owns the property and who needs to sign off on a sale.

You can find this out by searching your county’s land records office online (most counties have a free property search on their website) or by calling the county recorder’s office directly.

There are three common scenarios:

Only the incarcerated person is on the deed. You’ll need their legal authorization to sell — either through a Power of Attorney or a court order. Keep reading.

You and the incarcerated person are both on the deed. You still can’t sell without their consent or legal authorization. Joint ownership means both parties sign at closing. But the process is a bit simpler because you already have standing as a co-owner.

Only you are on the deed. You can sell normally. The incarcerated person has no ownership interest. (But check whether they’re on the mortgage — that’s a separate issue your title company can help with.)

Step 2: Get a Power of Attorney

In most cases, the fastest path to selling is getting the incarcerated owner to sign a Power of Attorney (POA). This is a legal document that gives someone else — usually a spouse, family member, or attorney — the authority to sign documents and make decisions on their behalf.

Here’s what you need to know about getting a POA signed in a correctional facility:

It has to be notarized. Most jails and prisons have a notary on staff or can arrange for one. Contact the facility’s administration office and ask about their process for legal document notarization. Some facilities have specific days or times for legal visits.

You’ll likely need an attorney to draft it. A general POA might not be enough. You want a specific or limited POA that explicitly grants the power to sell real property at a specific address. Title companies are more comfortable with this than a broad, general POA.

Call the facility first. Every jail and prison has different rules about what documents can be brought in, how visits work, and what their notarization process looks like. Some allow attorneys to visit outside normal visiting hours. Some require documents to be submitted in advance for review. A five-minute phone call to the facility saves you a wasted trip.

Pro tip: If you’re working with a cash home buyer (like us), they’ve usually handled this before and can coordinate the POA process with you. We’ve worked with families where the owner was in facilities across different states — the logistics are manageable once you know the steps.

Step 3: When a Power of Attorney Isn’t Possible

Sometimes a POA isn’t an option. Maybe the incarcerated person is uncooperative. Maybe they’re incapacitated. Maybe you can’t locate them within the system. In these cases, you may need to go through the courts.

A court order for sale is the alternative. You petition the court to grant you permission to sell the property. This is more common than you’d think — judges see these cases regularly, especially when there’s a mortgage in danger of foreclosure or the property is deteriorating.

Reasons a court will typically grant a sale order include the property being at risk of foreclosure, unpaid property taxes threatening a tax sale, the property being vacant and deteriorating, or the family needing the proceeds to cover essential expenses.

This route takes longer — usually a few months — and you’ll need a real estate attorney. But it works, and the court is generally sympathetic when a family is trying to do the responsible thing with an at-risk property.

Step 4: Deal with the Title

Here’s where a lot of these sales get stuck. Even with a POA or court order, some title companies won’t touch a transaction involving an incarcerated owner. They see it as too risky or too unfamiliar.

This is one of the biggest reasons traditional home sales fall apart in these situations. A regular buyer’s lender may refuse to finance the purchase because the title situation makes them nervous.

Cash buyers don’t have this problem. There’s no lender involved, no underwriter getting cold feet, and no financing contingency that can kill the deal. A cash buyer who’s experienced with complicated title situations can work directly with a title company that’s comfortable closing these transactions.

Step 5: Decide How to Sell

Once you have the legal authority to sell, you have the same options as any other homeowner — but your situation may make some options more practical than others.

List with an agent: This gets you the highest price on paper, but it takes time. You’ll need to show the property, wait for a buyer to get financing, and hope the deal doesn’t fall apart when the lender’s underwriter sees the title situation. If you have time and the house is in good shape, this can work. But if you’re behind on payments or the property is deteriorating, the clock is working against you.

Sell to a cash buyer: This is faster and simpler. No showings, no repairs, no financing contingencies. A reputable cash buyer can usually close in two to three weeks — sometimes faster. You’ll net less than a full-market listing, but you avoid months of mortgage payments, maintenance costs, and uncertainty. For most families dealing with an incarcerated owner, this is the path that makes the most sense.

What About the Mortgage?

If there’s a mortgage on the property, it still needs to be paid off at closing regardless of how you sell. The good news is that the sale proceeds cover this automatically — the title company pays off the existing mortgage before you receive your share.

If the house is worth less than what’s owed (you’re “underwater”), you may need to negotiate a short sale with the lender. This adds time and complexity, but it’s still better than letting the property go to foreclosure, which damages everyone’s credit — including the incarcerated owner’s.

How Long Does All of This Take?

It depends on which path you take. If the incarcerated owner is cooperative and you can get a POA signed, you could be at the closing table within three to four weeks of first contact. If you need a court order, you’re looking at two to four months for the legal process, plus the time to actually sell.

The biggest delays usually come from not knowing where to start and spending weeks or months doing nothing while the bills pile up. The sooner you talk to someone who’s handled this before, the sooner you can stop the bleeding.
