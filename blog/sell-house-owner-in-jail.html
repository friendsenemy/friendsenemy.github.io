How to Sell a House When the Owner Is in Jail or Prison

A practical guide for family members navigating the legal and financial complexities of selling a property when the owner is incarcerated.

Yes, you can sell a house when the owner is incarcerated, but it requires proper legal authorization. According to the Bureau of Justice Statistics, approximately 1.9 million people are incarcerated in the United States, many of whom own property that must be managed or sold by family members. The most common solutions are a durable power of attorney, a court appointment, or working with an authorized family member already on the deed. You’ll need to gather documents, work with an attorney, and navigate state-specific laws — but the sale itself is completely feasible. Here’s exactly how to make it happen.

Can You Sell a House If the Owner Is in Jail?

The short answer: yes. But it’s not as simple as listing with an agent and signing papers. The owner remains the legal property owner even while incarcerated, and the title is still in their name. You can’t sell someone else’s house without legal authority — but you have several paths to gain that authority.

The key is consent and documentation. If the incarcerated person wants the house sold and is willing to authorize you, the process becomes straightforward. If they’re unwilling or unreachable, you’ll need to pursue a court order or work through the probate system (if applicable).

How to Find Out Who Is on the Property Deed

Before you do anything, you need to know exactly who owns the property. The deed is the legal document that proves ownership, and it tells you who has the power to sell.

Here’s how to find the deed:

  • County land records office: Visit or call your Maryland county’s recorder’s office. Most have online databases where you can search by property address or owner name. The deed is public information.
  • Title company: An attorney or title company can pull a full title report showing ownership history and any liens or claims against the property.
  • Tax assessor records: Your county tax assessor’s office maintains property records and can tell you who is listed as the owner.
  • Online databases: Many counties offer free deed searches on their websites. A quick search usually takes 5 minutes.

Once you have the deed, you’ll see the owner’s name clearly. If the incarcerated person is the sole owner, you’ll need power of attorney or a court order. If there’s a co-owner (spouse, family member), that co-owner may have the right to sell their share or the whole property depending on how the deed is structured.

How to Get a Power of Attorney for an Incarcerated Person

A durable power of attorney is the gold standard. It allows someone (called an “agent”) to act on behalf of the incarcerated owner in all financial and property matters, including selling the home.

Here’s what you need to know:

  • The owner must execute it: The incarcerated person must sign the power of attorney document while in custody. They can work with a prison attorney, outside counsel, or a legal aid organization to draft it.
  • Notarization is required: Maryland law requires a notary public to witness and verify the signature. Many facilities allow notaries to visit, or the document can be processed through the prison administration. Some states and facilities are now offering virtual notarization.
  • Durable language is critical: The document must explicitly state it remains valid if the principal (incarcerated owner) becomes incapacitated. This is why you need an attorney — don’t use a generic template.
  • Timeline: With legal help, you can typically have a valid power of attorney within 1 to 3 weeks, depending on the facility and how quickly documents are processed.
  • Cost: Expect to pay $300 to $800 for an attorney to draft and oversee notarization, or less if you use legal aid (if the owner qualifies).

Once the power of attorney is signed and notarized, you have full authority to sell, sign documents, and close on behalf of the owner. This is usually the fastest and cleanest path.

Can You Sell a House Without the Owner’s Signature?

Not legally. The owner’s signature is required on the deed transfer and closing documents. However, you don’t need the owner to be physically present.

Options for signing without the owner present:

  • Power of attorney: You sign on their behalf with their authorization.
  • Electronic signatures and remote notarization: Maryland allows remote notarization via video conference. The owner can sign documents from their cell using a monitored facility computer or visiting room setup (if the facility permits).
  • Court-appointed conservatorship: If the owner is mentally incapacitated, a court can appoint a conservator to make financial decisions on their behalf.
  • Attorney-in-fact: Same as power of attorney — someone with legal authority to sign in the owner’s place.

The reality: most modern title companies and closing attorneys can work around the owner not being physically present, as long as you have the right legal authorization document.

How to Deal With Title Issues When the Owner Is in Prison

Here’s where things get complicated. Selling a house requires a clear title — meaning no claims, liens, or encumbrances. An incarcerated owner creates a few common title problems:

Mortgage or home equity loan: The debt doesn’t disappear because the owner is in jail. When you sell, the lender must be paid off from the proceeds. If the home is underwater (owes more than it’s worth), this can block a traditional sale. A cash buyer, however, can often purchase “as-is” even with a mortgage in place.

Tax liens or judgments: If the owner has unpaid property taxes, federal tax liens, or court judgments, these show up on the title. They must be paid from the sale proceeds, or a buyer must agree to take the property subject to the lien. Probate claims: If there’s an estate or will issue, creditors or heirs might have claims on the property. This is complex and requires legal counsel. See our article on what happens to a house with no will for more context.

Co-ownership disputes: If the property is jointly owned with a spouse or ex-spouse, both owners must consent to the sale. If there’s divorce or separation in play, this creates legal hurdles. Our guide on selling a house during divorce in Maryland covers this scenario.

Solution: A title company will run a full title search before closing and identify all of these issues upfront. Working with a cash buyer or distressed property specialist (like Pages of Purpose) who understands these title issues can save months of back-and-forth.

How Long Does It Take to Sell a House With an Incarcerated Owner?

Timeline varies based on your approach.

With a cash buyer (like Pages of Purpose): 14 to 21 days from offer to close. This assumes you already have power of attorney or co-ownership authority. If you need to obtain power of attorney first, add 2 to 4 weeks. Total: 4 to 6 weeks.

With a traditional real estate agent and financed buyer: According to the National Association of Realtors, the average home sale takes 45 to 60 days. Add 2 to 4 weeks for obtaining legal authority, plus potential delays if title issues arise or the buyer’s lender has questions about the incarcerated owner. Total: 3 to 5 months.

With court involvement: If you need a court-appointed conservator or guardian, add 6 to 12 weeks for the court process. Then add time for the sale itself. Total: 4 to 8 months or longer.

The key variables are: (1) whether you already have power of attorney, (2) how clean the title is, (3) whether you choose a cash buyer or financed sale, and (4) whether the incarcerated owner is cooperative and responsive.

What Happens to the Mortgage When the Owner Goes to Jail?

This is the question I get asked most often, and the answer is straightforward: nothing changes. The mortgage obligation stays in place.

Here’s what happens:

  • The debt doesn’t forgive: The lender doesn’t care that the owner is incarcerated. The monthly payment is still due.
  • The family or incarcerated owner is responsible: If the family wants to keep the house, someone must pay the mortgage. If no one pays, the lender will eventually foreclose.
  • The sale clears the mortgage: When you sell the house, the mortgage is paid off from sale proceeds. The owner (or their estate) gets whatever is left after the lender, taxes, and agent fees.
  • If the home is underwater: If the home is worth less than the mortgage balance, a traditional sale may not be possible. A cash buyer might still buy it, or the lender may negotiate a short sale.

What About Inherited Property or Partial Ownership?

Sometimes the incarcerated owner isn’t the sole owner. The property might be held jointly with a spouse, owned through an estate, or involve partial interests. These situations add complexity but are still solvable.

Jointly owned property (e.g., married couple): If the spouse is not incarcerated, they may have the right to sell their half or the whole property without the incarcerated owner’s permission, depending on how the deed was drafted (tenants by the entirety vs. tenants in common). Consult an attorney to know your rights.

Inherited property: If the incarcerated owner inherited the house and it went through probate, check the probate documents. You might need to follow probate procedures even for an incarcerated owner. Read more in our guides on selling inherited property in Maryland and what happens to a house when there’s no will.

Partial ownership: If the incarcerated owner owns only a portion of the property (e.g., 50% while another person owns 50%), both parties must consent to a sale. This is covered in detail in our article on selling partial interest in property in Maryland.

In these situations, the path forward depends on the exact ownership structure and the relationships involved. An attorney or title company can clarify your options. Steps to Selling the House: A Practical Roadmap

Step 1: Get the deed and understand ownership. Search your county’s land records to see who owns the property. Is it the incarcerated person alone, jointly with a spouse, or through a trust?

Step 2: Consult a Maryland real estate attorney. You need legal advice specific to your situation. An attorney can tell you what authorization is required and how to obtain it. Cost is usually $300 to $1,000 for a consultation and document preparation.

Step 3: Obtain power of attorney or court authorization. If the incarcerated owner will cooperate, pursue a durable power of attorney. If not, pursue a court-appointed conservatorship (longer, more expensive, but gives you legal control).

Step 4: Run a title search. A title company will identify any liens, mortgages, back taxes, or claims against the property. These must be addressed before closing. If there are liens or back taxes on the property, a cash buyer can typically handle those at closing.

Step 5: Decide on a sales method. Cash buyer for speed, agent for maximum price. Read our complete guide on how to sell your house fast in Maryland to compare your options. (Or a hybrid: get a cash offer as your safety net while you list with an agent.)

Step 6: Close the sale. Work with a closing attorney or title company. They handle all documents, arrange for proper signatures (with notarization if remote), and coordinate the funds transfer.

Special Considerations for Maryland

Maryland has specific rules about power of attorney, property transfers, and incarcerated persons.

  • Durable power of attorney: Maryland recognizes durable powers of attorney and they’re valid even if the principal becomes incapacitated or remains in custody.
  • Remote notarization: Maryland allows remote notarization for certain documents, which can help if the incarcerated person is in a facility that doesn’t allow in-person visits.
  • Recording requirements: Any deed or power of attorney must be properly recorded with the Maryland county recorder. Filing fees apply.
  • Homestead tax credit: If the owner qualifies for Maryland’s homestead tax credit, keep documents to prove the sale date, in case refunds or adjustments are owed.

What If the Incarcerated Owner Refuses to Cooperate?

This is harder, but still manageable in some cases.

If you’re a spouse or co-owner: You may have the right to sell the property or your share without the incarcerated person’s consent, depending on how the deed is structured and your marital status. If you’re going through a divorce, our guide on selling a house during divorce in Maryland explains your rights. An attorney can advise on your specific situation.

If you’re a child or other family member: You’ll likely need a court order. You can petition for a conservatorship or guardianship, which gives you control of the person’s financial matters (including the property). This takes 2 to 3 months in court and costs $2,000 to $5,000 in legal fees, but it works.

If the property is being abandoned or neglected: In some cases, you can pursue foreclosure or tax sale proceedings (slower but possible). You might also contact the incarcerated person’s attorney or a case manager to advocate for a power of attorney.
