What Happens to a House When the Owner Dies and There’s No Will?

A practical guide for heirs navigating probate, family disagreements, and selling an inherited property without a will.

Home › Blog › House With No Will

Published April 18, 2026 • Pages of Purpose LLC

Someone you love has passed away. They owned a house. And they didn’t leave a will. Now you’re standing in front of a property you might be responsible for — with no instructions, no clear owner, and no idea what happens next.

This is one of the most common situations I see as a real estate buyer, and it’s also one of the most confusing. People call me every week asking the same thing: “My mom died and there’s no will — can I even sell this house?” The answer is yes, you can. But you have to go through some steps first. Here’s what the process actually looks like.

First: The State Decides Who Inherits

When someone dies without a will, the legal term is “intestate.” It sounds clinical, but all it means is that instead of the person choosing who gets their stuff, the state follows a preset formula.

Every state has intestate succession laws — basically a priority list of who inherits. While the exact rules vary by state, the general order looks like this:

Surviving spouse gets all or most of the estate in almost every state. If there are also children, the spouse and children typically share it — the exact split depends on where you live.

Children (including adopted children) are next in line. If there’s no surviving spouse, the children usually inherit everything in equal shares.

Parents, then siblings, then more distant relatives come after that. The state keeps going down the family tree until it finds someone.

If there are absolutely no relatives, the property goes to the state. But this almost never happens — courts will search pretty hard for any living relative before that.

The key thing to understand: You don’t automatically own the house just because you’re the closest relative. The property has to go through probate first, and a court has to officially transfer ownership to you before you can sell.

What Is Probate (And Why Does It Take So Long)?

Probate is the court-supervised process of settling someone’s estate after they die. When there’s no will, the court needs to do a few things before anyone can touch the property: confirm who the legal heirs are, appoint someone to manage the estate (called an “administrator” when there’s no will), make sure any debts and taxes get paid, and then transfer the remaining assets — including the house — to the rightful heirs.

This process takes anywhere from a few months to over a year, depending on your state, whether anyone contests the process, and how complicated the estate is.

The most common holdup? Multiple heirs who don’t agree. When a parent dies without a will and there are three adult children, all three may have an equal claim to the house. If two want to sell and one doesn’t, things get complicated fast. More on that in a minute.

How to Get Appointed as Administrator

Since there’s no will naming an executor, somebody needs to step up and ask the court to appoint them as the estate’s administrator. This is usually the surviving spouse or the oldest child, but any interested party can apply.

Here’s the general process:

Go to your county probate court. You’ll file a petition to be appointed as administrator of the estate. The court clerk’s office can usually walk you through the paperwork — you don’t always need a lawyer for this step, though it helps.

The court will notify other potential heirs. Before appointing you, the court wants to make sure no one objects. They’ll send notice to other family members and sometimes publish a notice in a local newspaper.

You may need to post a bond. Some states require the administrator to post a surety bond — basically an insurance policy that protects the estate if the administrator mishandles things. The cost depends on the value of the estate.

Once appointed, you receive “Letters of Administration.” This is the document that gives you legal authority to act on behalf of the estate — including selling property. You’ll need this to close on any sale.

The Heir Problem: When Family Can’t Agree

This is where I see the most families get stuck. Mom or Dad dies, no will, and the house passes to three or four siblings who haven’t agreed on anything since 1997. One wants to sell. One wants to keep it. One lives out of state and won’t return calls. And one thinks the house is worth twice what it’s actually worth.

Sound familiar? Here’s the reality:

If the house passed to multiple heirs, you all own it. No single heir can sell the whole property without the others’ consent (or a court order). This is true even if one sibling has been paying the mortgage and maintaining the property for years.

A buyout is the simplest solution. If one sibling wants to keep the house, they can buy out the others’ shares at fair market value. This avoids the hassle of a public sale, but it requires the sibling who wants to keep it to have the money or qualify for a mortgage.

If you can’t agree, there’s a legal option called a “partition action.” Any co-owner can petition the court to force a sale. The court appoints someone to sell the property, and the proceeds get split according to each person’s ownership share. It works, but it takes time and costs money in attorney fees.

The fastest path when heirs disagree is usually a cash sale. Everyone signs off, the cash gets split on the spot at closing, and nobody has to deal with the property anymore. I’ve closed deals where siblings who hadn’t spoken in years were able to walk away with their share in less than a month.

What About the House Itself?

While probate is grinding along, the house still exists in the real world. And it still needs attention.

Someone needs to maintain it. If the house sits vacant with no heat in winter, the pipes burst. If nobody mows the grass, the city sends code violation notices. If the insurance lapses, one bad storm and you’re looking at an uninsured loss. The administrator is technically responsible for maintaining the property until it’s transferred or sold.

The mortgage doesn’t pause for grief. If there’s a mortgage on the property, the payments are still due. Lenders will give you some grace if you communicate early, but after a few missed payments, they’ll start foreclosure proceedings. I’ve seen families lose inherited properties to foreclosure simply because nobody made the calls early enough.

Property taxes keep accruing. Same story. Miss enough tax payments and the county can sell the house at a tax sale. This is especially common when the deceased person was already behind on taxes before they passed.

The bottom line: Even if you can’t sell the house yet, you need someone actively managing it. Pay the insurance. Keep the utilities on. Mow the lawn. These small costs protect a much larger asset.

The “Stepped-Up Basis” Tax Break You Should Know About

Here’s one piece of good news. When you inherit a house, the IRS gives you what’s called a “stepped-up basis.” This means that for tax purposes, the value of the house resets to whatever it was worth on the day the person died — not what they originally paid for it.

Why does this matter? Say your parent bought the house in 1985 for $60,000 and it’s now worth $200,000. If they had sold it while alive, they’d owe capital gains tax on that $140,000 gain. But because you inherited it, your “cost basis” is $200,000. If you sell it for $200,000, your capital gain is zero. You owe nothing.

This is a significant tax benefit, and it applies whether or not there was a will. Talk to a tax professional about your specific situation, but don’t skip this step — it could save you thousands.

How to Actually Sell the House

Once probate is complete (or far enough along that you have Letters of Administration), you can sell. Your options:

List with a real estate agent. This makes sense if the house is in good condition, you have time, and all the heirs agree. You’ll get the highest price, but you’ll also wait two to six months, pay for repairs, and pay agent commissions (typically five to six percent).

Sell as-is to a cash buyer. This is the route most families in this situation take, and honestly, it’s the one I’d recommend when the property needs work, the heirs want to be done with it, or you need to stop the financial bleeding from mortgage payments and taxes. No repairs, no showings, no waiting for buyer financing. You can close in as little as two to three weeks.

The house doesn’t need to be clean, updated, or even fully emptied. If Grandma’s furniture is still inside and nobody wants to deal with it, that’s fine. Cash buyers handle all of that.

What I’d Tell a Friend in This Situation

If a friend called me tomorrow and said “My dad just died and there’s no will — what do I do about the house?” — here’s what I’d tell them:

Don’t wait. Go to the probate court this week and start the process. The longer you wait, the more it costs you in mortgage payments, taxes, insurance, and maintenance on a property nobody’s living in.

Talk to your siblings early. Get everyone on the same page about whether to sell or keep the house before positions harden and resentments build.

Get a realistic number. Find out what the house is actually worth in its current condition — not what Zillow says, not what the neighbor sold for after a full renovation. What it’s worth right now, as-is.
