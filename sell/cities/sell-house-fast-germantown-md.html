Maryland & Montgomery County Rules That Actually Affect Your Sale

We’re not attorneys and nothing here is legal advice. But Germantown sellers lose money and time all the time because they didn’t know the specific rule applied to them. Here is the shortlist we walk through on every call.

Maryland Is a Judicial-Foreclosure State

Your lender cannot just auction your Germantown house. For owner-occupied residential property they must send a Notice of Intent to Foreclose at least 45 days before filing, then file an Order to Docket in the Montgomery County Circuit Court at 50 Maryland Avenue in Rockville, then serve you Maryland’s mediation notice, and only then schedule a Trustee’s Sale. The sale itself must be advertised for three consecutive weeks. After the auction there is a 30-day objection window before the court ratifies the sale. From NOI to ratification is often 6–9 months — but if you sell us the property before the sale date, the action is dismissed.

Probate at the Montgomery County Register of Wills (50 Maryland Avenue, Rockville)

Every Germantown estate goes through the Montgomery County Register of Wills at 50 Maryland Avenue in Rockville. Four main paths matter for a sale:

  • Small Estate — gross estate under $50,000 (or under $100,000 when the spouse is sole heir). Fastest path; often resolves in weeks.
  • Regular Estate — anything larger. Longer timeline, Personal Representative manages the sale.
  • Modified Administration — available when all residuary legatees qualify and consent. Streamlined accounting.
  • Summary Administration — limited situations, typically for very small estates owed to the surviving spouse or minor children.

You usually need Letters of Administration and court authority before you can actually close. We coordinate with your probate attorney and the Register directly.

MPDU — Moderately Priced Dwelling Units (30-Year Control Period)

Montgomery County’s Moderately Priced Dwelling Unit program is administered by the Department of Housing and Community Affairs (DHCA). Ownership MPDUs carry a 30-year resale price-control period measured from the original sale. During that control period:

  • The county holds a right of first refusal when you sell.
  • The resale price is restricted by a formula based on the original purchase price, the allowed annual appreciation, and approved capital improvements.
  • On the first sale after the control period expires, any profit above the formula price can be subject to a recapture payable to the county.
  • All of this must be coordinated through DHCA — the buyer cannot simply write any price.

If you don’t know whether your Germantown unit is still in its MPDU control period, send us the DHCA MPDU agreement and your deed, and we will tell you honestly what is and is not possible — including whether a direct cash sale makes financial sense once the resale formula is applied.

Montgomery County & Maryland Transfer & Recordation Taxes

Montgomery County’s transfer tax is 1.0% of the sale price. Maryland’s state transfer tax is 0.5%. Maryland recordation is $4.45 per $500 of consideration up to $500,000 and $6.75 per $500 above that. By Maryland custom, those are typically split between buyer and seller — on a cash-buyer sale to us, we absorb normal closing costs so the net figure on your settlement sheet is what you actually keep.

Montgomery County Annual Tax Sale

Montgomery County auctions delinquent tax liens each June. After the sale, the purchaser of the certificate must wait the statutory period, then file a complaint in the Circuit Court to foreclose your right of redemption. As long as that judgment has not been entered, you can still redeem the certificate and sell. We close fast and redeem at the settlement table out of the sale proceeds.

Ground Rent (§ 8-708) — Rare in Montgomery County

Registered ground rents are mostly a Baltimore-area artifact and are relatively rare in Montgomery County, but some older parcels on the east side of Montgomery County or along older rights-of-way carry one. Under Maryland Real Property § 8-708, the ground-rent holder must be registered with SDAT (by the 2007 registration cutoff and subsequent updates) to enforce it, and any unpaid rent has specific redemption procedures. If your title shows a ground rent, we deal with it at closing — you do not chase an out-of-state ground-rent holder.

§ 10-702 Disclosure or Disclaimer

Maryland’s Residential Property Disclosure/Disclaimer law (Real Property § 10-702) gives you a choice: a full Property Condition Disclosure, or a Disclaimer (sold as-is, no representations). Most of our Germantown cash sales are on the Disclaimer. Federal and state lead-based paint disclosures for homes built before 1978 are a separate, non-optional obligation.

Reduction of Lead Risk in Housing Act

Maryland Environment Article §§ 6-801 et seq. and federal Title X (42 U.S.C. § 4852d) both apply to pre-1978 residential property. Most Germantown housing stock was built 1985–2005, so the lead regime is the exception rather than the rule here — but older farmhouses along Clopper Road and Route 118, plus pre-1978 rentals owned by Germantown sellers, still trigger it. Lead-paint disclosure and the EPA pamphlet must be delivered at contract signing; registered rentals face additional MDE requirements. We handle all of it at settlement when it applies.

Maryland Condominium Act — Resale Certificates

If you are selling a Gunners Lake, Waters Landing, Germantown Town Center, or Milestone condo, the Maryland Condominium Act (Real Property § 11-101 et seq.) requires delivery of a resale certificate with specific financial and governance disclosures from the unit-owners association. The management company prepares it; we coordinate the request at the same time we open title so the packet doesn’t bottleneck your closing.

Post-Surfside Reserve-Study & Lender Questionnaire Pressure

After the 2021 Surfside collapse, Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and FHA tightened lender-questionnaire and project-approval rules for condominium projects. Buildings with funding gaps on capital reserves, pending major special assessments, or failed structural integrity reports can be flagged as non-warrantable, which kills conventional financing for retail buyers. As a cash buyer, we do not need the project to be Fannie-warrantable — we buy the unit, take on the assessment, and close.
