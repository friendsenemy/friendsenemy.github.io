Maryland Foreclosure Timeline in Harford County

Maryland is a judicial foreclosure state, meaning the lender must sue in the Circuit Court before conducting a trustee's sale. For Bel Air-area homes, that court is the Circuit Court for Harford County at 20 W. Courtland Street, Bel Air. Here is the sequence, roughly:

  1. Default & Loss Mitigation. After approximately 90+ days delinquent, the lender sends the Notice of Intent (NOI) to Foreclose — a statutory 45-day notice with a Loss Mitigation Application and foreclosure-mediation election form.
  2. Order to Docket. Once the NOI period expires, the lender files the Order to Docket in Harford County Circuit Court. Filing includes the Final Loss Mitigation Affidavit and a certified copy of the deed of trust.
  3. Foreclosure Mediation (optional). The borrower has 25 days after Order to Docket to request mediation through the Office of Administrative Hearings. Mediation typically adds 60 to 90 days.
  4. Trustee's Sale Scheduling. After 45 days post-Order to Docket (or after mediation closes), the trustee may schedule the auction, advertised in a newspaper of general circulation for three consecutive weeks.
  5. Auction. The sale occurs on the Harford County courthouse steps in Bel Air or at an appointed online platform. The successful bidder posts a deposit.
  6. Ratification. After a 30-day objection window, the court ratifies the sale. Only at ratification does legal title transfer. Before ratification, the borrower retains the right to sell.
  7. Surplus Funds. If the sale exceeds the debt, surplus goes to the borrower after the auditor's final report — usually months later and not automatic.

You can sell until ratification — that is the window we work in

Pre-auction and even post-auction (before ratification), we can often close and pay off the secured debt directly, saving your credit from the foreclosure entry. If you are reading an NOI or an Order to Docket right now, every day matters — call us before the auction date.

Maryland Probate & Inherited Bel Air Property

If you are the personal representative of an estate that includes Harford County real estate, your path through the Harford County Register of Wills (20 W. Courtland Street, Bel Air) depends on the size and composition of the estate:

Non-Probate Transfers

Property held as tenants by the entireties (spouses) or joint tenants with right of survivorship passes automatically to the survivor — no probate needed. A revocable living trust avoids probate entirely. Maryland recognizes payable-on-death designations for financial accounts but not for real estate — there is no Maryland "transfer-on-death deed" statute, so ownership of a Bel Air house cannot be passed by beneficiary designation.

Small Estate

Available when probate assets total $50,000 or less (or $100,000 or less if the spouse is the sole legatee). Streamlined paperwork, no Orphans' Court hearings, typically 2 to 3 months to close. Real estate sales are possible under Small Estate authority.

Regular Estate

For probate assets above $50,000 (or above $100,000 with spouse sole legatee). The personal representative posts bond, inventories assets within 90 days, and files an accounting every 6 months. Orphans' Court approval is required for most real estate sales unless the will grants the PR an independent power of sale. Typical timeline: 6 to 9 months to close the estate, but the real estate itself can often be sold mid-administration.

Modified Administration

A streamlined Regular Estate option when all residuary legatees consent in writing. Eliminates the accounting requirement and reduces Orphans' Court filings. A good fit for amicable families.

Transfer Taxes, Tax Sale, Lead, Ground Rent & Disclosure

Maryland & Harford County Transfer/Recordation Taxes

On a Bel Air sale expect: Maryland state transfer tax 0.5% (0.25% for first-time Maryland homebuyers, paid by seller by default), Harford County transfer tax 1.0%, Harford County recordation tax of $6.60 per $500 of consideration. By historical custom the transfer and recordation taxes are split equally between buyer and seller, but our cash offers absorb all seller-side transfer/recordation tax, title insurance, and typical closing costs — you net the offer price.

Harford County Tax Sale

Harford County holds its annual tax sale in June, auctioning tax-lien certificates on parcels with delinquent real property taxes, water and sewer liens, HOA liens that were properly recorded, and certain county liens. The certificate holder must wait 6 months (9 months for owner-occupied homestead) before filing a foreclosure of the right of redemption in Harford County Circuit Court. If your Bel Air property has been sold at tax sale but the certificate holder has not yet completed foreclosure, there is still time — we can redeem the certificate at closing and take clean title.

Ground Rent (Rare in Harford but Not Zero)

Ground rent is overwhelmingly a Baltimore City phenomenon, but a handful of older Harford County properties carry ground-rent leaseholds. Maryland's 2007 SDAT registration requirement extinguished unregistered ground rents under Md. Code Real Prop. § 8-708, but registered ones remain enforceable. We resolve ground-rent payoffs at closing when they exist.

Maryland Residential Property Disclosure / Disclaimer

Md. Code Real Prop. § 10-702 requires sellers to deliver either a Residential Property Disclosure Statement or a Disclaimer Statement. Estate sales, trustee sales, and some exempt transfers can use the disclaimer. We routinely accept disclaimer-based as-is sales. Latent defects known to the seller that could threaten health or safety still must be disclosed even under a disclaimer.

Maryland Lead-Risk Reduction Act

For rental properties built before 1978, the Reduction of Lead Risk in Housing Act (Environment Article §§ 6-801 et seq.) requires owners to register annually with MDE, pay the registration fee, and pass lead-risk-reduction inspections at tenant turnover. Non-compliance creates MDE liens and personal liability for elevated blood-lead cases. We take over the compliance burden on rental purchases. For owner-occupied homes the act does not apply, but federal Title X (42 U.S.C. § 4852d) lead-paint disclosure still does.

Harford County Health Department Well & Septic

For properties on private well and septic — most of Bel Air North, Forest Hill, Fallston, Jarrettsville, Whiteford, Pylesville, Churchville, and outer Bel Air — the Harford County Health Department maintains well-construction and septic-permit records. Financed buyers are required to obtain satisfactory water potability testing and septic inspection/percolation results. When those fail, financed deals die. We buy regardless.
